**Proposed Theoretical Model**

This model keeps the sites fixed, so we will use an LMM for the mixed model:

\begin{equation}
y_{ij} = \mu_{ij} + EU_i + \epsilon_{ij}
\end{equation}

The mean is the likelihood of being diagnosed with ADRD given the intervention arm (arm) and the booster (booster):

\begin{align}
\mu_{ij} \{ \text{ADRD} \mid \text{arm, booster} \} = &\beta_0 + \beta_1 I_{\text{arm=memory}} + \beta_2 I_{\text{arm=speed}} + \beta_3 I_{\text{arm=reasoning}} \notag \\
&+ \beta_4 I_{\text{booster=yes}} + \beta_5 I_{\text{arm=memory}} I_{\text{booster=yes}} \notag \\
&+ \beta_6 I_{\text{arm=speed}} I_{\text{booster=yes}} + \beta_7 I_{\text{arm=reasoning}} I_{\text{booster=yes}}
\end{align}

Where the indicators are defined as:

\begin{equation}
I_{\text{arm=memory}} =
\begin{cases}
1 & \text{if arm is memory} \\
0 & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\qquad
I_{\text{arm=speed}} =
\begin{cases}
1 & \text{if arm is speed} \\
0 & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
I_{\text{arm=reasoning}} =
\begin{cases}
1 & \text{if arm is reasoning} \\
0 & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\qquad
I_{\text{booster=yes}} =
\begin{cases}
1 & \text{if booster is yes} \\
0 & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

The random effects and error terms are distributed as:

\begin{equation}
EU_{i} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2_{EU}), \qquad \epsilon_{ij} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)
\end{equation}

Where $i = 1, 2, \ldots, 6$ for the six site locations, and $j = 1, 2, \ldots, 8$ is the combination of arm and boosters.

* Methods

ACTIVE protocol, power calculations(?) for targeted sample size, and stat analysis plan for clinical trial described elsewhere

ACTIVE study:
- four-arm multisite, single-blind randomized controlled trial
- N = 2802
- 26% minority diverse sample
- recruited from March 1998 to Octover 1999 in 6 metropolitan areas
- community-dwelling adults 65+ were eligible (factors in for medicare issue)
- Exclusion criteria:
  - signiifcant cognitive dysfuntion (Mini-mental state examination (MMSE) score < 23)
  - functional impairment (dependency or regular assistance in ADLs on Minimum Dataset Home Care)
  - Self-reported diagnoses of Alzheimers
  - stroke within last 12 months
  - certain cancers
  - current chemo/radiation therapy
    
- subjects randomized to one of the four intervention arms after completing baseline testing
- staff who administered cognitive testing and questionnaires were blinded to the intervention arms the participants were assigned to.
- no participant or public involvement in design, conduct, and reporting of trial

- Of 2802 ACTIVE subjecs, 2763 were matched to Medicare via Acumen's matching algorithm(?)"
  - 725 excluded, +8 who passed during enrollment
  - Final sample: 2021 subjescts (71% of original sample) roughly distributed across 4 arms.

** Cognitive Training intervention

Three intervention arms, 60-75min sessions in small groups over 5-6 weeks.
  - speed of processing training: visual search and ability to process increasingly complex info persented in successive shorter inspection times
  - Memory traniing: verbal episodic memory through instruction and practice in the use of e mnemonic strategies
  - Reasoning training: solving problems that contained serial pattern
  - Fourth arm was no contact control group
Booster training:
- needed at least 8/10 training sessions got randomized again
- sessions were 11 and 35 months after baseline
- 4 75min sessions
- booster is centrall to study design (prev studies shown booster sessions improved maintainance of training

** Medicare Claims Records

Medicare claims from 1/1/1999-12/31/2019 (20 years) were linked to training data for 2763 of the original 2802 subjects. Chronic Conditions Warehouse (CCW) algorithm diagnosed ADRD appearing in medicare claims. CCW algorithm demonstrated 85%-90% sensitive and specificity for diagnosing ADRD.

** Covariates

multiple prespecified covariates were included. vascular risks are associated with dementia so the analysis controled for cardiovascular risk factors. Although ACTIVE was a randomized trial. failure to adjust for covariates associated with an outcome could result in lower power, biased effect estimates, and inccorect conclusions.
Covariate data measured at baseline through ACTIVE or Medicare was completed for all matched subjects. 2 marital status was mssing, and baseline processing speed factor score had 16 missing. This study controlled for basic study design features by having fixed effects be the site and waves of training/testing (replicate training waves), an aspect of the study deisgn that eneabled the participants to be recruited and trained for ~ 1.5 years.

** Stat analysis

modified intention-to-treat analysis estimated a cause-specific Cox proportional hazard model: ADRD diagnosis and death. Casuse-specific hazard reported is the chance of diagnosis of ADRD. time scale was time since initial intervention
Schoenfeld residules were used to to test proportional hazard assumptions. If the assumptions were violated, they included interaction terms with covariates and time. Subjects wre censored if they enrolled in Medicare Advantage or reached end of study period without developing the end point of interest.

They reported both the cause-specific hazard models and the Fine-gray hazard models. Fine gray model estimates relative effect of intervention on cumulative incidence of outcome (diagnosis of ADRD) in the presense of a competing risk (death).

** Impact of each cognitive training intervention

Missing data among covariates were handled multiple (10 iteration) imputations. Standard Errors were adjusted for variablity introduced by multiple imputation according to Rubin's rule

Booster sessions:

*** Impact of baseline training on risk of ADRD
- 239/491 subjects (48.7%) in control wre diagnosed with ADRD within 20 year follow up.
- other arms tended to have lowre risk of being diagnosed with ADRD, but isnt statistically significant.

*** Impact of baseline training combined with booster sessions on risk of ADRD
- speed training+ randomized to recieve booster sessions had statistically lower hazard of diagnosed ADRD in unadjusted and adjusted models (adjusted HR: 0.74, 95% CI: 0.59-0.93)
- individuals in speed training arm + randomized booster sessions had lower hazard of diagnosed ADRD (adjusted HR: 0.81, 95% CI: 0.66-1.0) compared to speed training randomized that didnt get booster training.
- neither memory nor reasoning effects wre statistically different with booster training vs no booster training

*** impact of age at training on risk of ADRD

- no significatn effect of age at training on risk of diagnosed ADRD over 20 year follow up for participants in any of the three intervention arms.
- There was a trend (but not stat. significant) association of age for memory-trained group such that those whos started the memory intervention at a younger age experienced lower risk of ADRD
  - (**Age 65-69**: HR: 0.69, 95% CI: 0.46-1.02),
  - (**Age 70-74**: HR: 0.73, 95% CI: 0.51-1.05) compared to controls.

Results comparable to estimates based on fine-gray model.

** Discussion

- speed group who receieved booster training 11 and 35 months after baseline showed statistically significant lower risk of diagnosed ADRD over 20 year followup.
- subjects in speed group who did not complete initial speed training or any booster sessions did not show significantly lower risk of diagnosed ADRD
- no stat. sig. benefit of memory or reasoning training on diagnosis of ADRD

Speed training:
- repetitive task training in neurorehabilitation is well established in other neurological conditions like a stroke.
- possible reasons for this effect:
  1. dose effect: simply more training leads to better outcomes
  2. boosters strengthens training by adapting to the improving abilities of participant, as task difficulty increases. This is consistent with meta-analysis showing speed training effects are larger for tasks that are adaptive than once that aren't. This may suggest cognitive training needs to be repeated over time in a specific manner to reduce ADRD risk by facilitating the neuroplastic activation of neuronal connectivity.
- focused on improooving both visual processing and attention (particularrly divided attention).
- differedd from memory and reasining by being administered on a computer in an adaptive manner with increasing task difficult based on individuals performance (more curated).
- discussion focused on improved divided attention might show benefits on tasks like driving.
- speed training was associated with lower at-fault collisions 6 years after baseline, and number of speed training booster sessions was associated with maintenance of driving frequency. Other trainings shows impact on maintenance of driving, but speed was the greatest.


Post-randomization selection bias is possible becayse the study excluded certain people (werent matched, previous stroke, etc.). Medicare Advantage was excluded due to data limitations, but these subjects tend to be healthier as a group.

Match with medicare claim data mitigets study attrition and point-in-time measures of dementia status, bt theres still potential for selection bias in those who matches in medicae claims or erecieved diagnosis for ADRD. individuals who are able to access healthcare, subjects who have family who notice cognitive decline, and individuals with higher education are more likely to recieve dementia diagnoses. However, the strength of capturing cumulative incidence of dementia over 20 years from independently collected source would appear to outweigh these limitations.

Futher limitation is that booster training was offered to a random subset of subjectss in training groups who completed 8/10 initial sessions, but this was mitigated by comparing randomized booster to those eligible, but not randomized to booster.
